# KrabiClaw - Blog Strategy

## Strategy Name

**AI-Managed Local Presence**

KrabiClaw should own the category of local-business websites that are managed through ChatGPT and kept current from the owner's existing operating surfaces: Google Business Profile, Google Maps, Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, menus, reviews, photos, bookings, and analytics.

The blog should stop behaving like a generic small-business marketing blog. It should become the public operating manual for how local businesses use ChatGPT to keep their web presence accurate, searchable, and strategically active without learning a new CMS.

---

## Positioning

KrabiClaw is not just an AI website builder.

KrabiClaw is a website and marketing operating layer for local businesses where:

- ChatGPT is the primary creation and management surface.
- The website is SSR-rendered, SEO-first, and structured around local-business intent.
- Google Business Profile, Google Places, Facebook, Instagram, media, posts, menus, experiences, reviews, analytics, and translation are connected into the same system.
- Owners can keep their existing behavior: talk to ChatGPT, send WhatsApp updates, publish to social, or use the dashboard only when needed.
- Analytics can guide the next marketing move instead of sitting in a reporting tab nobody uses.

The blog should make this category obvious before readers know what to search for.

---

## Audience

Primary readers:

- Restaurant owners who rely on Google Maps, Instagram, Facebook, menus, reservations, and reviews.
- Experience operators such as tours, classes, workshops, studios, and activity businesses.
- Local business owners who already use ChatGPT and want it to do operational work, not just draft copy.
- Managed-service buyers who want better local SEO and AI discovery without learning another tool.

Secondary readers:

- Agencies and consultants serving local businesses.
- AI-search and local-SEO researchers comparing how local brands become visible in ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Perplexity, Claude, and similar answer surfaces.

---

## Editorial Thesis

The core thesis:

> Local businesses do not need another CMS. They need their real-world operating data turned into a living web presence that ChatGPT can update, analyze, and improve.

Every post should reinforce one of these beliefs:

- Google Maps is essential, but it is not a complete website.
- A restaurant menu should be crawlable HTML, not only a PDF or image.
- Reviews, Q&A, photos, hours, offers, and social posts should become reusable website content.
- Analytics should produce actions: blog posts, social posts, page updates, translation priorities, and seasonal campaigns.
- ChatGPT is strongest when it has tools, permissions, and live business data, not when it is asked to invent generic marketing advice.
- AI search visibility starts with useful, indexable, specific, people-first web content.

## SEO And AI Search Principles

Use normal SEO fundamentals as the base. Google's own guidance says generative AI features in Search are rooted in core Search ranking and quality systems, and there are no special technical requirements beyond being eligible for Search and snippets.

Useful references:

- [Google guide to generative AI features in Search](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/ai-optimization-guide)
- [Google AI features and your website](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/ai-features)
- [Google helpful, reliable, people-first content](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content)
- [Google LocalBusiness structured data](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/local-business)
- [Google Search Console generative AI performance reports](https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2026/06/gen-ai-performance-reports)

Operational rules:

- Write for real local-business decisions, not keyword variations.
- Prefer original KrabiClaw workflows, client examples, screenshots, teardown logic, and product-specific guidance over commodity SEO advice.
- Answer the target question directly in the opening section.
- Use clear headings, short sections, and concrete examples so retrieval systems can understand the page.
- Add FAQ or How-To structured components only when they genuinely help the reader.
- Use real platform media assets for featured images; do not invent image asset ids.
- Do not create `llms.txt`, special AI markup, fake citations, fake mentions, or pages whose only purpose is AI-search manipulation.

## Post Template

Every strategic blog post should include:

1. A direct answer in the first 80 words.
2. A concrete local-business example.
3. A clear explanation of what changes on the website, Google profile, social channels, or analytics workflow.
4. A KrabiClaw-specific workflow or product angle.
5. A practical checklist or decision framework.
6. Internal links to relevant product, blog, docs, or pricing pages when available.
7. SEO fields: `seo_description`, `seo_keywords` when useful, default `robots` unless a non-index behavior is intentional.
8. FAQ or How-To components only when the article has genuine question or process content.
9. A product-native CTA such as: manage this through ChatGPT with KrabiClaw.

Preferred shape:

```text
# Title

Short direct answer.

## Why This Matters
## The Old Way
## The ChatGPT-Managed Way
## What KrabiClaw Updates
## Example Workflow
## Checklist
## FAQ
## CTA
```

## Platform Blog Constraints

The current platform blog system supports:

- Public paths shaped as `/blog/<category>/<slug>`.
- Fixed platform categories from `utils/blog-categories.ts`: Marketing, Technology, Design, Business, SEO, Social Media.
- SEO fields: `seo_description`, `seo_keywords`, `canonical_url`, `robots`, and `featured_image_asset_id`.
- Structured components: FAQ and How-To through `components[]`.
- Component body embeds such as `{{component type="faq"}}` and `{{component type="how_to"}}`.
- Platform Admin MCP blog tools for list, get, create, update, publish, unpublish, and delete.
- Platform analytics via `get_platform_analytics`, including page views, sessions, visitors, top pages, and new signups.

Current content taxonomy is good enough for this strategy, but future cleanup should consider replacing generic categories with product-specific taxonomy such as Local SEO, ChatGPT CMS, Restaurants, Experiences, Analytics, and Case Studies.

## Stale Blog Cleanup Rules

Before writing new posts, audit existing published posts.

Delete or unpublish a post when:

- It is generic small-business or AI advice that could appear on any SaaS blog.
- It does not mention KrabiClaw, ChatGPT workflows, local SEO, Google Business Profile, social sync, analytics, restaurants, experiences, or managed service.
- It cannot be rewritten into one of the six pillars above.
- It targets an audience KrabiClaw does not currently serve.
- It creates inaccurate expectations about product behavior.

Rewrite a post when:

- The topic is strategically relevant but the angle is generic.
- It already has traffic or backlinks.
- It can be grounded in KrabiClaw's product, screenshots, real client examples, or analytics.
- It can become a pillar-supporting post without URL churn.

Keep a post when:

- It aligns with a pillar.
- It has useful traffic, signups, or internal-link value.
- It contains product-specific detail that would be expensive to recreate.

Deletion should happen through Platform Admin MCP `delete_platform_blog_post` only after the post has been reviewed. If there is any chance the URL has search value, unpublish or rewrite instead of deleting.

## MCP Workflow For Content Operations

Before any content operation:

1. Read `PRODUCT.md` or the `kc://docs/product-context` resource.
2. Use `get_platform_analytics` for the relevant period.
3. Use `list_platform_blog_posts` and `list_platform_docs` to understand existing content.
4. Fetch relevant posts with `get_platform_blog_post` before rewriting or deleting.
5. Draft for human approval before creating, updating, publishing, or deleting.

For new posts:

1. Pick the pillar and category.
2. Confirm target search intent and target reader.
3. Draft body, excerpt, SEO description, and optional structured components.
4. Use a real uploaded platform image if a featured image is needed.
5. Create as draft first unless explicitly approved for immediate publish.
6. Publish only after final approval.

For stale posts:

1. List all published posts.
2. Score each post against the cleanup rules.
3. Fetch the full body before making a keep, rewrite, unpublish, or delete call.
4. Prefer rewriting traffic-bearing URLs.
5. Report each action and rationale.

## Measurement

Track success by:

- Organic impressions and clicks for `/blog/*` pages.
- Generative AI feature impressions in Search Console when available.
- New signups influenced by blog and docs sessions.
- Internal clicks from blog posts to `/`, `/plugin`, pricing, docs, and contact flows.
- AI recommendation audits: repeated prompts in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, and Google AI Mode for restaurant website builders, ChatGPT local business websites, Google Maps synced websites, and restaurant SEO tools.
- Post-level conversions, not just page views.

Review cadence:

- Weekly during the first eight weeks of cleanup and publishing.
- Monthly after the first pillar set is live.
- Refresh or consolidate posts that do not earn impressions, links, signups, or internal-link value after a reasonable crawl and indexing window.

---

## Publishing Cadence

Initial push:

- Publish two strong posts per week for eight weeks.
- Prioritize category creation, Google Maps vs website education, restaurant SEO, and ChatGPT-managed workflows first.

Sustained cadence:

- Publish one strong post per week.
- Add one proof, teardown, or case-study post per month.
- Revisit analytics monthly and rewrite stale posts before adding more generic volume.

## Non-Goals

Do not use the blog to:

- Publish generic AI news.
- Chase broad small-business keywords unrelated to KrabiClaw's product.
- Create thin listicles.
- Publish speculative AI-search hacks.
- Duplicate docs content unless the blog post gives a strategic or buyer-facing angle.
- Promise platform capabilities that are not implemented.

---

## Editorial Bar

A post is ready only when it passes these checks:

- A local business owner can act on it.
- The opening answers a real question directly.
- The post contains KrabiClaw-specific product or operating detail.
- The post supports one of the six pillars.
- The post has a clear next step toward using KrabiClaw.
- The SEO description is explicit and useful.
- The category is valid for the current platform taxonomy.
- Any FAQ or How-To component is useful to humans, not just schema bait.
- No claim depends on a product integration, entitlement, or workflow that is not actually supported in `PRODUCT.md` or code.
